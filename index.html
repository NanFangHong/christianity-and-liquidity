Christianity and Liquidity

When people face sin, shame, debt, loneliness, failure, and death, the crisis often first appears as a liquidity crisis: no time, no trust, no margin, no way to begin again.

The Cross reveals the solvency crisis of human agency, but refuses final liquidation. The Church becomes the Spirit-enabled network where forgiven people learn to receive, repay, forgive, and start over.

The thesis

Human agency breaks in two familiar ways.

In prosperity, agency becomes proud and overlevered. In adversity, agency collapses into self-condemnation. The gospel names both as distortions of the self before God.

Prosperity misprices fragility

We treat temporary liquidity as permanent virtue, then call our advantage wisdom, discipline, or election.

Adversity misreads crisis

We mistake a liquidity break for final insolvency: I cannot continue, therefore I am beyond saving.

The Cross interrupts liquidation

Human sin is exposed without being allowed to become the final word about humanity.

Mere Christianity as container

Not a new denomination first. A shared diagnostic language.

Protestant, Catholic, Orthodox, Pentecostal, and mainline traditions often preserve different instincts for handling agency under pressure. This project treats those instincts as charisms to be translated, not trophies to be flattened.

Reformed

Checks overlevered pride by placing ultimate rescue in God's sovereign grace.

Catholic

Offers durable sacramental infrastructure for confession, penance, and repair.

Orthodox

Frames salvation as long healing, participation, and restored communion.

Pentecostal

Names the need for immediate help when a person cannot roll over another day.
